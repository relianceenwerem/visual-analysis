A note on method

How the images were read.

This is a comparative reading of five disaster photographs. The analysis proceeded in four stages: accessibility-compliant description, metadata extraction, cross-image pattern identification, and finally a two-axis relationship map.

The axes

Two axes structure the map. The horizontal axis distinguishes natural disasters from human-caused events — a meaningful distinction in how harm is understood and how response is organised, even though the line between the two is often less clean than it appears (flood severity is shaped by infrastructure decisions; terrorism plays out against natural geographies).

The vertical axis measures response capacity — well-resourced at the top, under-resourced at the bottom. This is read from the image itself: what aid infrastructure, equipment, personnel, and apparatus is visible in frame.

The patterns

Three patterns were identified inductively from the set itself, not imposed from a prior typology:

  • The response gap — a recurring mismatch between scale of destruction and capacity to respond, tracking closely (though not exclusively) with geography and event type.
  • Vertical witness — each image contains at least one surviving upright that anchors the frame and functions as ironic testimony.
  • Human as scale device — people in disaster photography frequently function less as protagonists than as instruments that make magnitude legible.

What the analysis is and isn't

This is not an attempt to rank disasters by severity, nor to compare suffering. The exercise is interested in how the photographs document events — what they make visible, what they leave implicit, what compositional choices recur across very different events.

Image 01 (9/11) is the only image that can be identified with certainty from visual content alone. The contextual attributions for images 02, 04, and 05 are inferred from architectural cues, landscape, clothing, and vegetation, and should be treated as probable rather than confirmed. Image 03 cannot be precisely dated or located without additional metadata.

A note on alt-text

Each image carries WCAG 2.1-compliant alt-text written to convey the depicted situation rather than the photographic qualities. Descriptions lead with the subject, include human and architectural scale markers where relevant, and avoid editorial language while preserving necessary contextual grounding. The alt-text is the entry point for screen reader users and was treated as primary text, not as a metadata afterthought.
